Assay Overview:
This assay involves culturing primary trabecular meshwork (TM) cells on polyacrylamide gels with precisely tuned substrate stiffness (4–80 kPa), thereby mimicking the mechanical environment ranging from healthy (softer) to glaucomatous (stiffer) tissue. The key measurement in this assay is the nuclear translocation of the YAP/TAZ transcriptional co-activators, which can be quantified using high-resolution fluorescence imaging and image analysis software. Essentially, the assay evaluates changes in mechanotransduction by determining the extent to which YAP/TAZ shift from the cytoplasm into the nucleus—a response that is well recognized to correlate with increased cellular contractility, fibrotic activation, and altered extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling. The biological material used is TM cells, which are pivotal in regulating aqueous humor outflow, and their biomechanical properties are directly linked to intraocular pressure regulation in glaucoma (karimi2024comparativeanalysisof pages 3-4, peng2018yapandtaz pages 7-8).

Biomedical Evidence:
The mechanobiological paradigm underlying this assay is central to glaucoma pathogenesis. In glaucoma, the trabecular meshwork exhibits pathological stiffening, which subsequently disrupts normal cell behavior by favoring nuclear localization of YAP/TAZ. This dysregulation leads to upregulation of fibrotic markers and increased contractility, both of which contribute to elevated outflow resistance and intraocular pressure—a major risk factor for glaucoma progression. Multiple studies have demonstrated that alterations in matrix stiffness directly modulate YAP/TAZ activity; for instance, glaucomatous TM cells respond more robustly to stiff substrates by enhancing actin cytoskeletal reorganization and increasing traction forces (karimi2024comparativeanalysisof pages 1-3, murphy2022matrixmechanotransductionvia pages 8-10). Moreover, the impact of glucocorticoids and growth factors like TGFβ2 on promoting YAP/TAZ nuclear translocation further underscores the relevance of this pathway in glaucoma pathology (li2022yaptazmediatetgfβ2induced pages 1-4, peng2018yapandtaz pages 7-8). Clinically, the correlation between increased TM stiffness and elevated intraocular pressure has been well documented, thereby validating the use of a mechanosensitive readout as a surrogate marker for disease state (nartey2021phenotypiccharacterizationof pages 109-116).

Previous Use:
This assay format has been effectively utilized to investigate the molecular and biomechanical underpinnings of glaucoma. Researchers have employed controlled substrate stiffness platforms to recapitulate TM cell behavior observed in glaucomatous eyes, showing that increased stiffness leads to amplified YAP/TAZ nuclear localization and enhanced traction force generation (karimi2024comparativeanalysisof pages 1-3, murphy2022matrixmechanotransductionvia pages 7-8). Furthermore, pharmacological interventions using agents like verteporfin, which inhibit YAP/TAZ, have been shown to normalize mechanotransductive signaling and reduce pathological TM cell contractility (li2022yaptazmediatetgfβ2induced pages 24-28, sung2023highvegfconcentrations pages 12-14). Such studies confirm that monitoring YAP/TAZ localization serves not only as a marker for mechanopathology but also as a functional readout for testing therapeutics aimed at restoring normal outflow dynamics.

Weaknesses:

• Variability in primary cell isolation and culture conditions could impact assay reproducibility and translation to clinical settings.
• While YAP/TAZ translocation is a robust mechanosensitive indicator, it represents only one aspect of the complex signaling network involved in glaucoma, and complementary assays (e.g., traction force microscopy) might be required for a comprehensive evaluation.
